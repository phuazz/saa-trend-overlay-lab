SAA Trend-Overlay Lab — Faber, over long history

Does a 10-month trend overlay help a nine-block strategic asset allocation? And is a per-asset overlay worth the complexity over one overlay on the balanced book?
VERDICT · KEEP SINGLE OVERLAY — EDUCATIONAL · NOT ADVICE
The verdict

Keep the single overlay — because the variants are statistically tied, not because per-asset lost.

Across 2004–2026 the plain base, the single overlay and the per-asset overlays are separated by less than one standard error — the per-asset-minus-single Sharpe gap has a bootstrap 95% interval of −0.13 to +0.36, a coin flip. When you cannot tell them apart, the simplest wins: one 10-month signal on the balanced book (1 signal versus 8, and far less turnover). And the overlay's durable contribution is drawdown, not return — a passive 60/40 matches it on Sharpe and beats it on compounding, so the ~1–2.4%/yr the overlay gives up is the price of a much shallower drawdown.

All figures are simulated backtests net of assumed costs — no live or paper track record exists. Personal educational research in a personal capacity; not investment advice, and not connected to any firm's products or services.

The concept, over long history

A Faber overlay holds each asset only while it is above its 10-month moving average, and sits in T-bills otherwise. Applied to US equity back to 1989 it gives up ~1.4%/yr of compounding but roughly halves the worst drawdown. The robust part is the drawdown: gating cuts the worst drawdown on nearly every sleeve, pre-ETF as well as post. The risk-adjusted-return edge, by contrast, is small and not statistically significant, and gold's 1983–2000 bear is a clear exception (see the table).

03

Weighting is the lever, not granularity

Give the base inverse-volatility weights and the single overlay C rises to meet the per-asset variants on the matched 2007–2026 window — evidence that per-asset overlays are largely a substitute for risk-balanced weighting. Treat this as a promising lead, not a conclusion: it is a single vol-window (36-month) result, concentrated in the 2008–14 bond bull, significant only at p≈0.035 before any multiple-testing haircut. It is the hypothesis for the next study, not an instruction to rewire weights.

    Method

    How it is built
    • Nine SAA blocks as spliced monthly total-return series: index / spot proxies, ETF bridges, and reconciled synthetic constant-maturity Treasury & T-bill series; deep sleeves reach 1962–1996.
    • No look-ahead: signal decided at each month-end, position lagged one month; out-of-market capital earns the spliced T-bill leg, not 0%.
    • Filter: 10-month SMA base; the 12-month robustness was run (it does not sustain the per-asset pass — see Robustness). Graduated variant E scales across a ±5% band. Equal-weight 12.5% base; inverse-vol as the robustness cut.
    • Costs: one-way, applied uniformly and swept 0/5/10/20 bps (decision at 10 bps); a per-class schedule was specified but not yet implemented. The 60/40 benchmark is shown gross. Turnover and whipsaw reported.
    • Window: deep sleeves run from their own starts (1962–1996); the nine-block comparison runs 2004-04 → 2026-06 (10-month warm-up + one-month lag from the 2003-05 EEM inception), stopping at the last completed month.
    • Tested: 14 unit tests including no-look-ahead in the assembly, cost accounting, and month/year boundaries.

    Every pre-ETF proxy is chained on returns and validated over the overlap window (β≈1, α≈0, high correlation). Synthetic Treasury series reconcile clean (corr 0.99). Derived outputs only — no vendor series values are shown (Norgate personal-use licence).
