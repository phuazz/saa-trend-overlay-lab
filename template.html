The concept, over long history

A Faber overlay holds each asset only while it is above its 10-month moving average, and sits in T-bills otherwise. Applied to US equity back to 1989 it gives up a little compounding but roughly halves the worst drawdown. The mechanism is not a post-2008 artefact — it is visible in the pre-ETF proxy era on every sleeve that reaches back far enough.

The finding — weighting is the lever, not granularity

Give the base inverse-volatility weights (risk-parity-lite) and the single overlay C catches up entirely: on a matched 2007–2026 window, risk-balancing lifts C by about +0.20 Sharpe — a pure weighting effect — and the per-asset advantage vanishes. Per-asset overlays were largely a substitute for risk-balanced weighting.

Method & caveats

How it is built
  • Nine SAA blocks as spliced monthly total-return series: index / spot proxies, ETF bridges, and reconciled synthetic constant-maturity Treasury & T-bill series; deep sleeves reach 1962–1996.
  • Signal decided at each month-end, position lagged one month (no look-ahead); out-of-market capital earns the spliced T-bill leg, not 0%.
  • 10-month SMA base (12-month as robustness); equal-weight 12.5% base, inverse-vol as the robustness cut.
  • One-way costs swept 0/5/10/20 bps; decision referenced at 10 bps.
